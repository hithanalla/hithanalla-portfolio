Overview

Linespect is a Sausalito-based environmental tech startup building HABForecastr — a submersible microscope that uses AI and deep learning to predict harmful algal blooms 7 days in advance. Similar to a weather app forecast, but for deadly algae instead.

During my time there, I chose not to stay in a narrow lane and built out the company's full go-to-market function — market research, investor pitch, product pricing, content strategy, website build, and stakeholder outreach. At a 6-person startup, everyone needed to be an expert in their own field. So, as the only person facing anything customer-side, I wore many hats.

The core challenge: how do you price, position, and market a first-of-its-kind environmental sensor to federal agencies, aquaculture operators, and water managers — with no playbook?

Before I could answer why this price, why this position, or why this lab over that one, I had to build the foundation those answers would eventually sit on. Leadership believed we were ready to go-to-market, but it was clear to me we were still in an ideation stage — so my first job wasn't to have the answers yet, it was to define the framework that would hold them once we did. That meant establishing a clear framework:

Part One — Market Research

The Question

Which 10% of algae species cause 90% of the damage?

Before Linespect could train its device or pitch investors, we needed to know which HAB species actually mattered — commercially, not just scientifically. Out of 45+ documented species, which ones should the device be built to detect first? The answer would decide the product roadmap, the sales pitch, and who we'd sell to.

I assumed this would be a quick literature review. It became a three-month lesson in what happens when you try to quantify something that has never been quantified before.

Dead End #1

Asking AI for the numbers

Like most people would today, I turned to AI tools to speed up the research — scite.ai and Perplexity, hunting for papers with hard economic-impact figures on health, fisheries, and tourism.

⚠️

What went wrong: when I checked the underlying papers, the numbers didn't exist. AI was citing sources that never said what it claimed they said.

The lesson wasn't "don't use AI" — it was that AI is a guide, not a source, and for a niche enough question, it will fill gaps with numbers that sound plausible and aren't real. I still recommend it for prompt-engineering your way to the right search terms. You just have to go read the actual paper.

Reframing

The question that actually worked

If AI was inventing numbers, it probably meant the research simply didn't exist in a clean, comparable form — not that I was using the wrong tool. So I asked for help instead of asking harder: I emailed a Northeastern professor and the author of a NOAA article directly. Both responded.

The key thing I learned from them: HABs aren't time-series events. They're sporadic, regionally inconsistent, and don't follow a predictable annual pattern — which is exactly why nobody had built a single clean economic ranking of them. That meant I needed a different variable entirely. I landed on bloom frequency as a proxy for economic importance:

Frequency became the stand-in for impact: more of the first drove more of the second, which is what made the third measurable at all.

Dead End #2

The database that looked like a shortcut

In this process, I came across a database (HAEDAT) — at the time, just a jumbled CSV file — logging bloom events across the US. I thought I'd hit the jackpot.

Figure 1 — bloom events mapped and color-coded by causative species. The idea was to see which algae persisted across the past 30 years.

I visualized it in Tableau, categorized by species on a map, hoping to narrow down which algae had persisted over that time — since that would tell me what actually mattered. But there were too many subcategories to make sense of the frequency visually.

So I switched gears and analyzed it by count per species instead. That narrowed things down significantly — a shortlist of the top 10, and naturally, the next step from there was the top 4.

Figure 2 — bloom count by causative species. This is what actually produced a usable shortlist, rather than the map view.

⚠️

Insight: after reading through the papers, I realized frequency alone wasn't a fair weight. The severity of a single event could outweigh 20 smaller blooms, and the cost itself was inconsistent, since impact varies from city to city. In Florida, for example, Karenia brevis hits tourism, jobs, and seafood all at once, while a cyanobacteria bloom might only affect property values around a small-town freshwater lake.

With so many variables at play, I chose to keep it simple and rely on published numbers. Comparing Tableau against the papers, I drew my conclusion based on published impact — which is why this ranking shifts. You can see the resulting top 4 in the blog series I published.

Why this mattered: this wasn't just a research exercise — it became the sales playbook. If a beta tester was in Long Beach, WA, we could open the conversation with their specific bloom history and dollar losses instead of a generic pitch. That's the difference between "we have a cool sensor" and "we know exactly what this has already cost you."

Part Two — Industry Research

The Gap

What is HABForecastr actually competing against?

Before I could position HABForecastr, I needed to know what it was being positioned against. I built a competitive matrix, a perception map, and a customer segmentation to inform our entry to the market.

The gap: nearly every existing player measures a proxy for bloom conditions — dissolved oxygen (DO), photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), or fluorescence — but not phosphate. Almost nobody in the market was measuring it directly. Customers wanted a high-quality product at a cheaper price than the competition.

Part Three — Building a Website

The Constraint

Build the whole company site with $0.

On top of the research, I designed and built the SubMicroScope website from scratch — hand-drawn wireframes to a live, DreamHost-hosted domain — without spending.

01

Wireframed every page

Sketched the homepage, blog hub, tech page, contact page, and careers page on paper — right down to which elements needed a "learn more" link, where a form should sit relative to a booking calendar, and which icons should look clickable. Getting the logic right on paper meant fewer wasted hours in Elementor later.

The actual paper wireframe — purpose, context, and element notes worked out before touching Elementor.

02

Learned WordPress the hard way, on a local host

Built the site locally with XAMPP before ever going live — the recommended workflow, and the one that saved me when things broke. I once renamed a config file to keep my sites organized (wp-config_scope.php instead of wp-config.php) and made the whole site unrecognizable to the server. Lesson: don't get clever with WordPress's naming conventions.

03

Chose plugins by trial, error, and elimination

Elementor for design, W3 Total Cache and WP-Optimize for speed, RankMath for SEO. Some choices took real failure to reach: All-in-One Migration undercounted file sizes and kept failing on deploy, so I switched to UpdraftPlus. WPForms was too limiting, so I moved to Fluent Forms for its free conditional logic.

04

Cut load time from 2.9s to 0.9s

Caching, database optimization, and disabling pingbacks/trackbacks (which also hardened the site against DDoS traffic) got the homepage from a sluggish 2.9 seconds down to 0.9.

05

Built a webhook pipeline

Connected all 3 site forms — general contact, datasheet request, and product interest — to Google Sheets in real time via Make.com. Each form needed its own logic:

  • Contact form: keeps every entry (duplicates included, since one visitor might have two separate questions)
  • Interest and datasheet forms check for an existing email first and either skip or overwrite it, so the list never balloons with duplicates
06

Small changes that make the website great, not good

A confetti animation fires when a form submits — a tiny detail, but it took a script in the theme's footer file, custom JS in Fluent Forms, and a comp-sci teammate double-checking a piece of code we were both reading backwards. Added hCaptcha to every form after spam concerns, and hard-coded away template elements the starter theme wouldn't let me remove any other way — it was just one line of code:

/* Additional CSS — Appearance → Customize */ .[element-class-found-in-inspector] { display: none !important; }
07

Ran 4 rounds of user testing, including one outsider

The most useful insight came from testing with a mechanical-engineering teammate versus testing it on myself. I, a business student, read the device breakdown top-down — camera to lightsheet. She read it bottom-up, starting from where the lightsheet enters the water. Same page, opposite reading order. We restructured the tech page to work for both.

The other user insight that changed the site: people only filled out the main contact form if they already intended to buy. Everyone else — the just-curious, the not-yet-ready — needed a lower-commitment way in. That's why the site ended up with three separate forms instead of one: awareness-stage visitors and ready-to-buy visitors don't want the same box to fill out.

Content Strategy

8 blogs, one methodology, a real audience.

The blog series turned the market research into something three audiences could use at once: SEO content to drive organic traffic, plain-language education for buyers who'd never heard the term "HAB," and a sales reference the field team could quote from directly. Every article traced back to a real paper, a real dataset, or a direct source — not a summary of a summary.

The series opened with the foundational piece — what a HAB actually is, and why 375 were recorded across the U.S. in 2022 alone — before splitting into the top-4-species breakdown and its downstream effects on human health, aquaculture, finfish, shellfish, shoreline property, and climate.

Reflection

What I actually learned.

Startups have grim days and good ones, and no one needed me to go beyond mediocre — the praise was the same either way. So, what kept me pushing on the details, the afterthoughts, the long-term experience, was smaller than that: the title of my Google Doc, "Best Website I Ever Created." It became an affirmation more than a file name. Life lesson: sometimes a Google Doc title can help you through your off days.

The most valuable thing this co-op taught me wasn't a skill — it was a mindset. When AI gave me bad data, I called a NOAA researcher. When a data visualization didn't work, I built a new methodology from scratch. For me, it was about figuring it out.

Working directly with the mechanical and bioengineering teams also changed how I think about product marketing. You can't position something you don't understand at a technical level — and writing content for the tech page with a mechanical engineer taught me that. For example, we both visualized the direction of light in opposite directions — her functionally, mine structurally. That became a real reminder that "clear" is relative to who's reading it.
