Overview

One co-op. Founding team output.

Linespect is a Sausalito-based environmental tech startup building HABCamera — a submersible microscope that uses AI and deep learning to predict harmful algal blooms 7 days in advance. Think of it as a weather app, but for water.

As the Business Development Co-op, I didn't have a narrow lane. I owned the full go-to-market function — market research, investor pitch, product pricing, content strategy, website build, and stakeholder outreach. At a 6-person startup, everyone wears every hat. I wore most of them.

The core challenge: How do you price, position, and market a first-of-its-kind environmental sensor to federal agencies, aquaculture operators, and water managers — with no existing market playbook to follow?

Market Research

Finding the 10% of algae that cause 90% of the damage.

The goal was deceptively simple: figure out which algae species matter most commercially, so we could train the device and target the right customers. The execution was anything but simple.

My first instinct was to use AI to pull economic impact data. Every chatbot gave me made-up numbers citing papers that didn't say what they claimed. Dead end. That failure forced a better methodology.

01

Reached out directly to experts

Emailed a Northeastern professor and a NOAA article author directly. Both responded. Learned that HABs aren't time-series events — they're sporadic and uncertain, which meant economic data was fragmented by region and incident rather than species.

02

Reframed the question entirely

Instead of chasing a single economic value, I asked: what drives economic impact? The answer was bloom frequency. More blooms = more papers = more documented losses. Frequency became the proxy for importance.

03

Visualized 30 years of HAEDAT bloom data

Pulled the international HAB event database, attempted to visualize by decade in Tableau. Too many subcategories — hit a dead end. Pivoted to tracking specific incidents and cross-referencing NOAA regional articles instead.

04

Built the final species ranking

Narrowed 45+ species down to 4 — Cyanobacteria, Karenia brevis, Pseudo-nitzschia, and Alexandrium catenella — covering roughly 90% of US bloom activity and documented economic impact. Turned this into a blog series, a competitive sales guide, and investor pitch content.

The research methodology I built — frequency as a proxy for economic importance, cross-referenced against NOAA regional data — became the foundation for the product's target market and the sales playbook for beta testers.

Website & Tech

Built the whole company site for $0 in tools.

On top of the research and strategy work, I designed and built the SubMicroScope website from scratch — from hand-drawn wireframes to live deployment on DreamHost. The constraint: use nothing that costs money.

01

Wireframed every page by hand

Sketched 5 full page layouts — homepage, tech page, blog hub, careers, and contact — with annotated design decisions before touching WordPress.

02

Built on WordPress.org + Elementor

Local host development first, then deployed to DreamHost. Optimized load time from 2.9s to 0.9s using W3 Total Cache and WP-Optimize. Fixed a broken config file with the engineering team when the domain change broke site access.

03

Built a webhook automation system

Connected 3 contact forms to Google Sheets via Make.com webhooks — with duplicate-email logic, conditional routing, and hCaptcha security. All free tools, all working in production.

04

4 rounds of user testing

Ran structured user tests including one external (non-employee) tester. Key insight: technical and non-technical users navigate the same page in opposite directions. Redesigned the tech page layout accordingly.

05

Grew to 8K monthly visitors

Built and executed a content strategy using RankMath SEO analytics. Published the 8-part HAB blog series as the primary organic traffic driver.

Content Strategy

8 blogs. One research methodology. A real audience.

The blog series served three functions simultaneously: SEO content to drive organic traffic, educational material for potential customers who didn't know what a HAB was, and a sales tool for the field team to reference when pitching to specific regions.

Each article was grounded in primary research — real papers, real data, real citations. The writing made complex environmental science accessible without dumbing it down.

Reflection

What I actually learned.

The most valuable thing this co-op taught me wasn't a skill — it was a mindset. When AI gave me bad data, I called a NOAA researcher. When WordPress broke, I dug into the config file. When the data visualization didn't work, I built a new methodology from scratch. Early-stage startups don't reward the person who knows everything. They reward the person who figures it out.

Working directly with mechanical and bioengineering teams also changed how I think about product marketing. You can't position something you don't understand at a technical level. Taking lightsheet microscopy images alongside the engineering team wasn't in my job description — but it made every piece of content and every pitch more credible.

"Early-stage startups don't reward the person who knows everything. They reward the person who figures it out."

— Hitha Nalla, Linespect Co-op 2025
