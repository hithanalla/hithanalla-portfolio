Case Study 03

Taking a gut-health soda
from zero to launch.

Role

Overview

A new product in a crowded category. A full GTM from scratch.

Pepsi Pure is a concept gut-health soda — a new product entry into the functional beverage space where brands like Olipop and Poppi have been quietly building serious market share. The challenge: how does a legacy brand like Pepsi credibly enter a category that's built its identity around being the anti-Pepsi?

As Marketing Lead on a cross-functional team of 5, I owned the go-to-market strategy end to end — market analysis, customer segmentation, positioning, channel strategy, and the financial model behind it all.

The core tension: Pepsi has massive distribution and brand recognition, but the gut-health consumer actively distrusts big CPG. The GTM had to solve for credibility without abandoning scale.

Market Analysis

Understanding the category before building the strategy.

The functional beverage space is growing fast but unevenly. Gut-health specifically has moved from niche health food stores to mainstream grocery — driven by younger consumers who treat digestive health as a daily routine, not a medical concern.

01

Mapped the competitive landscape

Analysed Olipop, Poppi, Kevita, and existing Pepsi product lines. Key finding: the winning brands in this space lead with ingredient transparency and founder story — neither of which Pepsi has naturally. That shaped the entire positioning strategy.

02

Identified the whitespace

Existing gut-health sodas cluster around the "health food" consumer. There's an underserved segment of mainstream soda drinkers who want healthier options but find current offerings too niche, too expensive, or too "wellness-coded." Pepsi Pure targets that gap.

03

Sized the real opportunity

The gut-health soda subsegment is growing at ~15% CAGR. Pepsi's existing distribution network gives it a structural advantage no startup can replicate — if the product is positioned correctly to not alienate the health-conscious early adopters who'd otherwise influence mainstream adoption.

Customer Segmentation

Three segments. Three different reasons to buy.

Rather than targeting "health-conscious consumers" as a monolith, I identified three distinct segments with different motivations, price sensitivities, and channel preferences.

🏃
The Wellness Converter
Already drinks Olipop or Poppi. Sceptical of Pepsi. Needs ingredient credibility and clean label. Won't be won on price — only on formula transparency.
🛒
The Mainstream Upgrader
Current Pepsi or Coke drinker who's been meaning to "drink better." The biggest volume opportunity. Won on accessibility, familiar taste, and a price point that doesn't feel like a health tax.
👪
The Family Buyer
Buying for the household, not themselves. Wants something the whole family will drink that isn't pure sugar. Won on value pack availability and school/family-safe positioning.

Positioning Strategy

The insight that shaped everything.

Every competitor in the gut-health space positions against legacy soda — "the better alternative," "what soda should have always been." Pepsi Pure can't play that game. It IS legacy soda.

The reframe: instead of distancing from the Pepsi brand, Pepsi Pure leans into it. "You already trust Pepsi. Now Pepsi trusts science." It turns the brand's biggest perceived liability — corporate scale — into a proof point for formula rigour and safety that a small startup can't credibly claim.

Positioning line: "The taste you know. The gut you've been waiting for." — Familiar enough to convert mainstream buyers. Specific enough to not alienate health-curious consumers.

Channel Strategy

Where and how to launch.

Launch sequencing matters as much as the product itself. A mismatched channel at launch can permanently damage perception before word-of-mouth builds. The strategy was phased: earn credibility in health channels first, then use Pepsi's distribution muscle to scale.

🌿
Phase 1 — Health Retail
Whole Foods, Sprouts, Target health aisle. Builds credibility with Wellness Converters before mainstream rollout.
📱
Phase 1 — DTC + Social
Direct-to-consumer with influencer seeding. Micro-influencers in gut health and nutrition — not celebrity deals.
🏪
Phase 2 — Mainstream Grocery
Walmart, Kroger, Costco value packs. Deploy Pepsi's distribution network once health-channel credibility is established.
🍽️
Phase 2 — Food Service
Restaurant and café channels. Positions Pepsi Pure as the premium option at the point of order — higher margin, higher visibility.
🤝
Phase 3 — Co-branding
Partnerships with gut-health platforms and nutritionists. Adds third-party credibility that the Pepsi brand can't self-generate.

Financial Modeling

The numbers behind the strategy.

Every strategic recommendation was backed by a financial model showing the cost, projected revenue, and break-even timeline. The model was built to be conservative — assuming slower-than-average health segment adoption and accounting for the premium pricing resistance from mainstream buyers.

Year Channel focus Est. units Revenue Net position
Year 1 Health retail + DTC launch 180K cases $3.2M $(1.4M)
Year 2 Mainstream grocery rollout 520K cases $9.1M $(0.3M)
Year 3 Full distribution + food service 1.1M cases $19.4M $2.8M
Year 4 Co-branding + international test 1.8M cases $31.5M $7.2M

Note: figures are projection estimates built for academic strategy purposes, based on comparable beverage category launch data.

Reflection

What I took away from it.

The most interesting tension in this project was the brand liability problem. Pepsi's scale is simultaneously its biggest asset and its biggest obstacle in this category. Working through that forced me to think about positioning not just as messaging, but as a sequenced market entry problem — you have to earn the right to be in certain channels before you can unlock others.

Building the financial model also taught me that strategy without numbers isn't really strategy. Every recommendation I made had to survive the question: "what does this cost and what does it return?" That discipline has shaped how I approach every project since.
