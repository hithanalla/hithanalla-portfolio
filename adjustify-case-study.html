Overview

Nobody asked me to build this. That's exactly why I did.

Adjustify is a budgeting tool I conceived, designed, and prototyped from scratch — starting with a problem I noticed, through user research, to a full Figma prototype. No client brief. No professor prompt. Just a gap I spotted and the instinct to fill it.

Most budgeting apps are built around tracking what you already spent. Adjustify is built around adjusting in real time — the idea that your budget shouldn't be a report card for last month, it should be a live tool that helps you make decisions today.

The insight: People don't fail at budgeting because they don't care. They fail because most budgeting tools show you what went wrong after it's too late to do anything about it.

The Problem

What's broken about budgeting apps right now.

I started by talking to people around me — friends, classmates, family — about how they actually manage money. The pattern was consistent: almost everyone had tried a budgeting app, and almost everyone had stopped using it within a few weeks.

What users said

"I check it at the end of the month and feel bad"
"It just tells me I overspent — doesn't help me fix it"
"Too many categories, I give up setting it up"
"I forget to check it so it becomes useless"
"It makes me anxious so I avoid it"

What Adjustify solves

Real-time budget status — not just end-of-month reports
Actionable suggestions: "move $40 from dining to cover this"
Simple setup — 3 categories to start, add more later
Gentle nudges mid-month, not guilt at month-end
Calm, non-judgmental visual language throughout

Design Process

From napkin sketch to Figma prototype.

01

Talked to real people first

Before opening Figma, I had informal conversations with 8 people about their budgeting habits. The goal wasn't a structured user interview — it was understanding the emotional relationship people have with their finances. Most apps design for the rational user. Real people are emotional about money.

02

Defined the core use case narrowly

Most budgeting apps try to do everything — tracking, goals, investments, subscriptions. Adjustify does one thing: helps you adjust your spending in real time when you're heading off track. Scope constraint as a design principle, not a limitation.

03

Sketched the core flows by hand

Onboarding, the main dashboard, and the "I'm overspending" intervention flow were sketched on paper first. Getting the logic right before touching Figma meant I didn't waste time designing something that didn't make sense to use.

04

Built the full prototype in Figma

Designed every screen — onboarding, dashboard, category breakdown, adjustment flow, and settings. Used a soft, calm colour palette deliberately — most finance apps use red for "over budget" which triggers anxiety. Adjustify uses amber and soft greens to signal "heads up" rather than "you failed."

05

Tested with the same people I interviewed

Brought the prototype back to three of the original eight people. Key feedback: the adjustment suggestion feature was the most liked — and the onboarding had one step too many. Iterated on both before considering the prototype complete.

Design Decisions

The intentional choices behind the UI.

Colour palette

Soft purples and warm neutrals — finance apps default to blue (trust) or red (alert). Adjustify uses lavender to feel approachable, not corporate, and amber instead of red for warnings.

Information density

One number at a time — the dashboard shows your most important number front and centre. Category detail is one tap away, never forced on the user at first glance.

Onboarding length

3 steps max — most budgeting apps lose users in setup. Adjustify gets you to a working budget in under 2 minutes by starting with just three spending categories.

Language tone

Encouraging, never judging — "You've got £40 left in dining this week — want to adjust?" not "Warning: dining budget exceeded." Same information, completely different emotional response.

Reflection

What building your own product teaches you.

Every other project in this portfolio had a client, a brief, or a team. Adjustify had none of those — just a problem I noticed and a blank Figma canvas. That changes everything about how you work.

Without a client to validate decisions, you have to validate them yourself — which means talking to users earlier, testing assumptions more honestly, and being willing to throw away a design you spent hours on because it didn't hold up in practice. The onboarding iteration after user testing is a small example of that: I was attached to the 4-step flow, and the feedback was clear it needed to be 3. It did.

🔍
Scope is a design decision
Doing one thing well beats doing everything badly. The hardest part of Adjustify was deciding what to leave out.
💬
Emotion drives product adoption
People don't abandon budgeting apps because they're hard to use. They abandon them because they feel bad using them. Design for the feeling, not just the function.
🔄
Attachment is the enemy of iteration
The best design decision I made was throwing away the 4-step onboarding I'd spent hours on. User feedback doesn't care about your sunk cost.
🚀
Starting is the whole trick
Self-initiated work doesn't wait for permission. The fact that it exists at all is the point — it shows you create outside of what's asked of you.
